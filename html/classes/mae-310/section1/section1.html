Introduction

Heat Transfer is thermal energy in motion due to a temperature difference

Key Units of Measurement:
  • Thermal Energy (U or u) has the units of J or J⁄kg
  • Temperature (t) has the units of K or ºC
  • Heat (Q) has the units of J
  • Heat Rate (q) has the units of W
  • Heat Flux (q") has the units of W⁄m2
Three modes of heat transfer:
  • Conduction:
    • Heat transfer in a solid or stationary fluid due to the random motion of it atoms, molecules, or electrons
    • Requires the presence of a temperature difference in a material medium
    • An example is heat transfering from a hot pan to cook food laying on the top of the pan
  • Convection:
    • Heat transfer due to bulk and random motion for a fluid flow over a surface
    • Typically transfers from a solid (the surface) to a moving fluid
    • Requires the presence of a temperature difference in a material medium
    • An example is a laptop fan blowing air on a hot computer chip to lower the chip's temperature
  • Radiation:
    • Energy that is emitted by matter due to changes in electon configurations
    • Transported as electromagnetic waves as photons
    • Transport does not require a material medium and is most efficient in a vacuum
    • An example is the sun giving off solar radiation to heat Earth

Heat Transfer Rates

Conduction can be calculated using Fourier's Law:
  • q" = -k∇T
    • q" is the heat flux in W⁄m2
    • k is the thermal conductivity in W⁄m*K
      • Higher values mean that the heat trasnfers quicker. This is why metal feels colder than plastic even when they are the same temperature
    • ∇T is the temperature gradient in ºC⁄m or K⁄m
      • ∇Tx = T2 - T1 ⁄ Lx
  • q = q" * A
    • q is the heat rate in W
Convection can be calculated using Newton's law of cooling:
  • q" = h(Ts - T∞)
    • q" is the heat flux in W⁄m2
    • h is the convection heat transfer in W⁄m2*K
      • Changes with the velocity and the substance that the moving fluid is composed of
    • Ts is the temperature of the surface in ºC or K
    • T∞ is the temperature of the moving fluid in ºC or K
Radiation can be calculated using emission and absorption equations
  • Emission (E) is the release of heat due to radiation
    • E = εEb = εσTs4
      • E is the emissive power in W⁄m2
      • ε is the surface emmissivity (0 ≤ ε ≤ 1)
      • Eb is the emmissive power of a black body (a perfect emitter)
      • σ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant and is equal to 5.67 * 10-8 W⁄m2*K4
  • Absorbed Incident Radiation (Gabs) is the absorption of heat due to radiation
    • Gabs = αG
      • Gabs is the absorbed incident radiation in W⁄m2
      • α is the surface absorptivity (0 ≤ α ≤ 1)
      • G is the irradiation in W⁄m2
