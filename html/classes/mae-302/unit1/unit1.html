Gas power cycles

Thermodynamics I Review

Simple Compressible Substance are subject to compression/expansion as well as heating/cooling.
  • Only two independent thermodynamic properties are needed to determine its state for a pure substance
Pure substances are made up of a single type of molecule such as (H2O). Air can be considered a pure substance.

Systems are a substance with mass and volume and is defined by its boundaries.
  • Closed Systems can exchange heat/work but not mass
  • Open Systems can exchange heat/work as well as mass
Energy is the ability to do work. It can be stored through internal energy, kinetic energy, and potential energy

Reversible Work is frictionless and restrained expansion and can be returned to its original state.

Cycles

What are cycles? Cycles are a set of connected processes that return to the original state. They allow for continous operation. heating/cooling.

Heat Engines produce work from heat input.
  • Kelvin-Planck statement is the need reject heat to sustain a net work output. This means that heat input needs to be greater than the desired work output.
  • Recieve heat from a high-temperature souce.
  • Convert part of the inputted heat to work
  • They reject remaining waste into a low-temperature sink
Energy Analysis and Thermal Efficiency:
  • Assume the cycle can be considered closed
  • Assume no energy is stored (net input equals net output)
  • nth = Desired Output⁄Required Input = Wnet, out⁄QH = 1 - QL⁄QH
Carnot Cycle: an ideal cycle in which every process is reversible
  • Isothermal (constant temperature) expansion/heat addition
  • Reversible and adiabatic (no heat transfer) expansion (Isentropic Expansion)
  • Isothermal compression/heat rejection
  • Reversible and adiabatic compression (Isentropic Compression)
  • nth, carnot = Desired Output⁄Required Input = 1 - QL⁄QH
  • 0 ≤ nth ≤ nth, carnot ≤ 1
  • ∆s = 0
Reciprocating Engines are a single device (piston-cylinder)
  • Otto (internal combustion)
  • Diesal (internal combustion)
Steady Flow Devices have multiple components in steady flow
  • Brayton – Gas turbine cycle (Internal Combustion)
  • Rankine – Steam or 2-phase (External Combustion)
Air Standard Cycles are cycles in which the following assumptions are applicable.
  • The working fluid is air, which continuously circulates in a closed loop and always behaves as an ideal gas
  • All the processes that make up the cycle are internally reversible
  • The combustion process is replaced by a heat-addition process from an external source
  • The exhaust process is replaced by a heatrejection process that restores theworking fluid to its initial state

Reciprocating Engines

Net work output:
  • Wnet = MEP * Apiston * stroke = MEP * Displacement Volume
Compression Ratio:
  • r = Vmax ⁄ Vmin = VBDC ⁄ VTDC
  • TDC is the top of the piston stroke while BDC is the bottom of the stroke
Mean Effective Pressure:
  • MEP = Wnet ⁄ Vmax - Vmin (kpa)
Energy Analysis:
  • Ufinal - Uinitial = ∆U = Qin - Wout
  • Equations of State:
    • pV = mRT or pv = RT
      • p is the absolute pressure
      • T is the absolute temperature
      • V is the volumes
      • v is the specific volume
      • m is the mass
      • R is the gas constant
  • Aproximations for h and u:
    • ∆h = cp, 300 K(T2 - T1)
    • ∆h = cp, avg(T2 - T1)
    • ∆u = cv, 300 K(T2 - T1)
    • ∆u = cv, avg(T2 - T1)
  • Isentropic Relations: (reversible and adiabatic work)
    • For constant specific heats (cold air assumption)
      • T2 ⁄ T1 = (v1 ⁄ v2)k - 1
      • p2 ⁄ p1 = (v1 ⁄ v2)k
      • T2 ⁄ T1 = (p2 ⁄ p1)k - 1⁄k
      • k = cp ⁄ cv > 1

Otto Cycle

Otto Cycle is a reciprocating spark ignition engine.

Process 1 → 2:
  • Isentropic compression from BDC to TDC
  • win (kJ⁄kg) = u2 - u1
    • For constant specific heats: win = cv(T2 - T1)
  • Isentropic constraint where s1 = s2
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T2⁄T1 = (V1⁄V2)k-1 = rk-1
      • p2⁄p1 = (V1⁄V2)k = rk
    • For variable specific heats:
      • r = V1⁄V2 = Vr1⁄Vr2
      • p1⁄p2 = pr1⁄pr2
Process 2 → 3:
  • Constant volume heat addition at TDC
  • qin (kJ⁄kg) = u3 - u2
    • For constant specific heats: qin = cv(T3 - T2)
Process 3 → 4:
  • Isentropic expansion from TDC to BDC
  • wout (kJ⁄kg) = u3 - u4
    • For constant specific heats: wout = cv(T3 - T4)
  • Isentropic constraint where s3 = s4
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T3⁄T4 = (V4⁄V3)k-1 = (V1⁄V2)k-1 = rk-1
      • p3⁄p4 = (V4⁄V3)k = (V1⁄V2)k = rk
    • For variable specific heats:
      • r = V4⁄V3 = Vr4⁄Vr3
      • p4⁄p3 = pr4⁄pr3
Process 4 → 1:
  • Constant volume heat rejection at BDC
  • qout (kJ⁄kg) = u4 - u1
    • For constant specific heats: qout = cv(T4 - T1)
Net Work Output:
  • Wnet, out = wout, 3-4 - win = qin - qout = (u3 - u2) - (u4 - u1)
    • For constant specific heats: wnet, out = cv(T3 - T2) - cv(T4 - T1)
Thermal Efficiency:
  • nth = 1- qout ⁄ qin = 1 - u4 - u1⁄u3 - u2
    • For constant specific heats: nth, Otto = 1 - 1 ⁄ r k - 1

Diesel Cycle

Diesal Cycle is the ideal cycle for compression-ignition engines.
  • Only air is compressed during the compression stroke eliminating the possibilty of autoignition (engine knock).
  • Can operate at much higher compression ratios than spark ignition engines (typically between 12 and 24).
  • The spark plug in spark ignition engines is replaced with a fuel injector.
  • Cut-off ratio rc = v3 ⁄ v2 = T3 ⁄ T2
Process 1 → 2:
  • Isentropic compression from BDC to TDC
  • win (kJ⁄kg) = u2 - u1
    • For constant specific heats: win = cv(T2 - T1)
  • Isentropic constraint where s1 = s2
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T2⁄T1 = (V1⁄V2)k-1 = rk-1
      • p2⁄p1 = (V1⁄V2)k = rk
    • For variable specific heats:
      • r = V1⁄V2 = Vr1⁄Vr2
      • p1⁄p2 = pr1⁄pr2
Process 2 → 3:
  • Constant pressure heat addition at TDC
  • qin - wout, 2-3 = u3 - u2 (kJ⁄kg)
  • qin = h3 - h2
    • For constant specific heats: qin = cp(T3 - T2)
  • wout, 2-3 = R(T3 - T2)
Process 3 → 4:
  • Isentropic expansion from cut-off to BDC
  • wout (kJ⁄kg) = u3 - u4
    • For constant specific heats: wout = cv(T3 - T4)
  • Isentropic constraint where s3 = s4
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T3⁄T4 = (V4⁄V3)k-1 = (r⁄rc)k-1 = rk-1
      • p3⁄p4 = (V4⁄V3)k = (r⁄rc)k
    • For variable specific heats:
      • (r⁄rc)k = V4⁄V3 = Vr4⁄Vr3
      • p4⁄p3 = pr4⁄pr3
Process 4 → 1:
  • Constant volume heat rejection at BDC
  • qout (kJ⁄kg) = u4 - u1
    • For constant specific heats: qout = cv(T4 - T1)
Net Work Output:
  • Wnet, out = wout, 2-3 + wout, 3-4 - win = qin - qout = (h3 - h2) - (u4 - u1)
    • For constant specific heats: wnet, out = cp(T3 - T2) - cv(T4 - T1)
Thermal Efficiency:
  • nth = 1- qout ⁄ qin = 1 - u4 - u1⁄h3 - h2
    • For constant specific heats: nth, Diesel = 1 - 1 ⁄ r k - 1(rkc - 1 ⁄ rc - 1)

Brayton Cycle

Brayton Cycle is the simple ideal cycle for gas-turbine engines.
  • The combustion process is replaced by a constant-pressure heat addition from an external source.
  • The exhaust process is replaced by a constant-pressure heat rejection to the ambient air.
  • Pressure ratio: rp = p2 ⁄ p1 = p3 ⁄ p4

Performance Variables, Win = ∫vdp
  • The goal is to maximize the work output by operating with as high of a temperature as possible between the two pressures.

Assumptions:
  • Treated as open systems
  • Steady flow (no storage of energy or mass)
  • ∆KE = ∆PE = 0
  • Ideal Gas

Process 1 → 2:
  • Isentropic compression in a compressor
  • Ẇin = ṁ(h2 - h1)
  • win (kJ⁄kg) = h2 - h1
    • For constant specific heats: win = cp(T2 - T1)
  • Isentropic constraint where s1 = s2
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T2⁄T1 = (V1⁄V2)k-1
      • p2⁄p1 = (V1⁄V2)k
      • T2⁄T1 = (p2⁄p1)(k-1)/k = rp(k-1)/k
    • For variable specific heats:
      • r = V1⁄V2 = Vr1⁄Vr2
      • p1⁄p2 = pr1⁄pr2

Process 2 → 3:
  • Constant pressure heat addition
  • Q̇in = ṁ(h3 - h2)
  • qin = h3 - h2
    • For constant specific heats: qin = cp(T3 - T2)
  • qin = cp(T3 - T2)

Process 3 → 4:
  • Isentropic expansion in a turbine
  • ẆT = ṁ(h3 - h4)
  • wout = h3 - h4
    • For constant specific heats: wT = cp(T3 - T4)
  • Isentropic constraint where s3 = s4
    • For constant specific heats:
      • T3⁄T4 = (V4⁄V3)k-1
      • p3⁄p4 = (V4⁄V3)k
      • T3⁄T4 = (p3⁄p4)(k-1)/k = rp(k-1)/k
    • For variable specific heats:
      • V4⁄V3 = Vr4⁄Vr3
      • rp = p3⁄p4 = pr3⁄pr4

Process 4 → 1:
  • Constant pressure heat rejection at BDC
  • Q̇out = ṁ(h4 - h1)
  • qout = h4 - h1
    • For constant specific heats: qout = cp(T4 - T1)

Net Work Output:
  • Wnet, out = wout - win = qin - qout = (h3 - h2) - (u4 - u1)
    • For constant specific heats: wnet, out = cp(T3 - T2) - cv(T4 - T1)

Thermal Efficiency:
  • nth = 1 - qout ⁄ qin = 1 - h4 - h1⁄h3 - h2
    • For constant specific heats: nth, Brayton = 1 - 1 ⁄ rp(k-1)/k

Development of Gas Turbines:
  • Increasing the turbine inlet (or firing) temperatures.
  • Increasing the efficiencies of turbomachinery components (turbines, compressors).
  • Adding modifications to the basic cycle (intercooling, regeneration or recuperation, and reheating).

Deviation from actual vs idealized:
  • Irreversibilities in turbine and compressors, pressure drops, heat losses.
  • Isentropic efficiencies of the compressor and turbine:
    • nc = ws ⁄ wa = h2s - h1 ⁄ h2a - h1
    • nT = wa ⁄ ws = h3 - h4a ⁄ h3 - h4s
    • ws = wisentropic
    • wa = wactual
  • Use the ideal states and isentropic efficiencies to determine the actual states.
  • Note that starting states (1 and 3) are fixed. There is no ideal or actual states for them.

Improving Thermal Efficiency:
  • If we increase the pressure ratio, at a certain rp, T2 > T4
  • If T2 < T4, we can use part of qout to reduce the required qin. This process is called regeneration.
  • Intercooling is the insertion of cooling stages between different stages of compression.
  • Reheating is the insertion of heating stages between different stages of expansion.

Regeneration:
  • Only valid if T2 < T4 and is most effective at lower pressure ratios and lower min to max temperature ratios.
  • High-pressure air leaving the compressor can be heated by the hot exhaust gases leaving the turbine by using a counter-flow heat exchanger. Doing so increases the heat of the gas before reaching the combustion chamber.
  • qin, with regen = qin, w/o regen - qregen = h3 - h5
  • qout, with regen = qout, w/o regen - qregen = h6 - h1
  • qregen = h4 - h6 = h5 - h2
  • Underneath ideal regeneration, T4 = T5', T2 = T6'
  • Underneath ideal regeneration and an ideal gas, h4 = h5', h2 = h6'
  • Underneath ideal regeneration, qregen, ideal = h4 - h2
  • Generator effectiveness: ε = qregen ⁄ qregen, ideal
  • nth, regen = 1 - (T1 ⁄ T3)(rp)(k - 1)/k
    • Only valid for constant cp

Intercooling, Reheating, and Regeneration:
  • P2 ⁄ P1 = P4 ⁄ P3
  • P6 ⁄ P7 = P8 ⁄ P9 = √(rp)
  • Compressor: wc = (h2 - h1) + (h4 - h3) = 2(h2 - h1)
  • Turbine: wt = (h6 - h7) + (h8 - h9) = 2(h6 - h7)
  • Heat Addition: qin = (h6 - h5) + (h8 - h7)
  • Heat Rejection: qout = (h2 - h3) + (h10 - h1)
  • Regeneration: qregen = h9 - h10 = h5 - h4
  • Thermal Efficiency: nth = 1 - qout ⁄ qin
