Finite Control Volume Analysis

Finite Control Volume Analysis

Basis of Finite CV Analysis:
  • Conservation of Mass.
  • Newton's Second Law of Motion.
  • 1st and 2nd Laws of Thermodynamics.

Conservation of Mass:
  • Key to tracking flowing fluid.
  • Based on amount of fluid entering and leaving a control volume.

Newton's Second Law of Motion:
  • Concludes that forces can result from or cause changes in fluid velocity magnitude and/or direction.
  • Torque can result in a flowing fluid’s moment-of-momentum change.
  • Help’s analyze work and power transfer of the fluid.

1st Law of Thermodynamics.:
  • Conservation of energy, energy cannot be created or destroyed.

2nd Law of Thermodynamics.
  • Clarifies the loss of useful energy associated with every actual process.
  • Laws help analyze work/losses in devices.

The Continuity Equation

Conservation of Mass:
  • DMsys⁄Dt = 0
  • By RTT, D⁄Dt ∫sys ρ dV= ∂⁄∂t ∫cvρ dV + ∫csρ(V * n̂) dA
    • (V * n̂) dA is the volume flow rate through dA.
    • ρ(V * n̂) dA is mass flow rate through dA.
    • V * n̂ is the component of velocity perpendicular to dA and is positive for flow out of the CV.

For a fixed non-deforming C.V.,
  • ∂⁄∂t ∫cv ρ dV + ∫csρ(V * n̂) dA = 0
  • ∫csρ(V * n̂) dA = Σ ṁout - Σ ṁin

For a moving non-deforming C.V.,
  • V = W + Vcv
    • W is the fluid velocity as seen from the observer moving with the control volume.
    • Vcv is the velocity of the control volume from a fixed point.
    • V is the fluid velocity with respect to the fixed point.
  • DMsys⁄Dt = ∂⁄∂t ∫cv ρ dV + ∫csρ(W * n̂) dA

For a deforming C.V.,
  • V = W + Vcs
    • W is the fluid velocity with respect to the fixed point.
    • Vcv is the velocity of the control volume from a fixed point. This non-uniform due to the c.v. deforming.
    • V is the fluid velocity with respect to the fixed point.
  • DMsys⁄Dt = ∂⁄∂t ∫cv ρ dV + ∫csρ(W * n̂) dA = 0

The Linear Momentum Equation

Newton's Second Law:
  • Any reference or coordinate system that fits the second law is said to be inertial (not accelerating or deforming).
    • Fixed, non-deforming c.v.
    • Moving but inertial, non-deforming c.v.
  • Dsys⁄Dt ∫sys VρdV = ΣFsys

Moving, Intertial, Non-Deformign C.V.,
  • ∂⁄∂t ∫cv (W + Vcv)ρ dV + ∫cs(W * Vcv)ρW * n̂ dA = ΣFcontent of the cv
  • For a constant c.v. velocity (Vcv) and a steady flow rate:
    • ∫csWρW * n̂ dA = ΣFcontent of the cv

The Moment-of-Momentum Equation

The Moment-of-Momentum Equation: relates torque and angular momentum of the flow.
  • Used when torques are important.
  • Predominantly used to solve dynamics of machines that rotate about a single axis (turbomachines).
  • D⁄dt ∫sys(r x V)ρ dV = ∂⁄∂t ∫cv(r x V)ρ dV + ∫cs(r x V)ρV * n̂ dA
  • ∂⁄∂t ∫cv(r x V)ρ dV + ∫cs(r x V)ρV * n̂ dA = Σ(r x F)

Generic Interpretation:
  • Tshaft = (-ṁin)(±rinVθin) + ṁout(±routVθout)
  • Ẇshaft = (-ṁin)(±UinVθin) + ṁout(±UoutVθout)
  • wshaft = (±UinVθin) + (±UoutVθout)

First Law of Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics Equation
  • D⁄dt ∫syseρ dV = (Q̇net in + Ẇnet in)sys
    • e is the total stored energy per unit mass for each particle.
    • e = û + V2⁄2 + gz
    • Q̇net in is the net rate of heat transfer into the system.
    • Ẇnet in is the net rate of work transfer into the system.
  • ∂⁄∂t ∫cveρ dV + ∫cseρ(V * n̂) dA = (Q̇net in + Ẇnet in)cv

For a steady single fluid stream entering and leaving a c.v.,
  • ṁ[ûout - ûin + (p⁄ρ)out - (p⁄ρ)in + V2out - V2in ⁄ 2 + g(zout - zin)] = Q̇net in + Ẇshaft net in
  • ṁ[ĥout - ĥin + V2out - V2in ⁄ 2 + g(zout - zin)] = Q̇net in + Ẇshaft net in
  • If flow is truly steady throughout such that no work is done, is 1D, and involves a single fluid stream, then Ẇshaft net in = 0
