Introduction

Fluid Mechanics

What is a fluid? Fluids are substances with the ability to flow. If stress is applied to a fluid it will modify its shape. Gases and liquids are considered to be be fluids while solids are not.

What is fluid mechanics? Fluid mechanics is the study of fluids in motion (dynamics) as well as fluids at rest (statics). Fluid mechanics allows for a better understanding of internal flow whether it be through liquid cooling or blood pumping through the heart.
  • Internal Flow: the flow of fluids within a system such as water being transported through tubes or pumps
  • External Flow: the flow of fluids around a system such as a boat in water or a plane moving throuth the air

Units

Systems of units: Fluid mechanics employs both SI (Metric) and IU (Imperial) systems of units. There are four primary units used
  • Mass (m):
    • SI: kilogram (kg)
    • IU: slug
    • 1 slug = 14.6 kg
  • Length (l):
    • SI: meter (m)
    • IU: feet (ft)
    • 1 ft = .305 m
  • Time (t):
    • Both SI and IU use seconds (s)
  • Temperature (T):
    • SI: Kelvin (ºK)
    • IU: Rankine (ºR)
    • 1 ºK = 1.8 ºR

Dimensional Homogeneity

An equation is dimensionally homogenous if all the units are the same when adding two things together or cancel out when multiplying and dividing. If an equation is not dimensionally homogenous, it is more than likely incorrect.

Bernoulli's Equation:
  • P + ρv2 + ρgz = Constant
  • This is dimensionally homogenous as all the parts of the equation can be expressed with the same unit ( kg⁄m*s2 ).

Density

Density (ρ) is defined as the fluid's mass per unit volume. The value of density varies between different fluids and varies slightly for liquids at different pressures and temperatues. Most liquids however are considered incompressible meaning that their density doesn't change. Gases are highly compressible meaning that they often change in density based on temperature and pressure.

For ideal gases:
  • p = ρRT
    • p is the absolute pressure (measured relative to a vacuum).
    • R is the gas constant (287.1 J⁄kg*ºK ).
    • T is the temperature (measured in ºK).
Density Related Properties:
  • Specific Volume:
    • v = 1⁄ρ ( m3⁄kg )
  • Specific Weight:
    • γ = ρ * g ( N⁄m3 )
  • Specific Gravity:
    • SG = ρ⁄ρwater
    • Generally at 4ºC where ρwater = 1000 kg⁄m3
A liquid when poured into a graduated cylinder is found to weigh 8 N and occupies a volume of 500 ml. Determine its specific weight, density, and specific gravity.
  • m = w⁄g = 8 N⁄9.81 m⁄s2 = .8155 kg
  • v = 500 ml = .0005 m3
Specific Weight:
  • γ = ρ * g = 1631 kg⁄m3 * 9.81 m⁄s2 = 16000 N⁄m3
Density: 1631 kg⁄m3
  • ρ = m⁄v = .8155 kg⁄.0005 m3 = 1631 kg⁄m3
Specific Gravity:
  • SG = ρ⁄ρwater = 1631 kg⁄m3⁄1000 kg⁄m3 = .6131

Viscosity

Viscosity (µ) is a fluid's internal resistance to flow. It is highly dependent on the fluids temperature.
  • τ = µdu⁄dy (kg⁄m*s or N*s⁄m2)
  • Fluid Velocity:
    • Next to moving plate, u = U
    • Next to fixed plate, u = 0
    • Middle between plates, u = u(y)
Newtonian Fluids are fluids in which the shearing stress is linearly related to the shearing strain.

Non-Newtonian Fluids are fluids in which the shearing stress is not linearly related to the shearing strain. The slope of the curve is denoted as the apparent viscosity µap.
  • Shear Thinning Fluids are fluids when the apparent viscosity decreases with increasing shear rate
  • Shear Thickening Fluids are fluids when the apparent viscosity increases with increasing shear rate
No-slip Condition is common for liquids and gases and causes the velocity of liquids to slow when closer to a fixed surface. This make the velocity distribution of Newtonian fluids parabolic with the highest velocity in the middle of the two plates.
  • v = 3U⁄2 [1 - (y⁄h)2]

Compressibility of Fluids

Bulk Modulus (Ev) is the property to characterize the compressibility of the fluid. Ev = p for incompressible fluids and Ev = kp for compressible fluids
  • Ev = -dp⁄d∀/∀ = dp⁄dρ/ρ
    • dp is the differential change in pressure
    • d∀ is the differential change in volume
    • ∀ is the volume
When gases are compressed or expanded the relationship between pressure and density depends on the nature of process.
  • Isothermal Process (constant temperature): p⁄ρ = constant
  • Isentropic Process (frictionless and no heat exchange): p⁄ρk = constant
    • k is the ratio of specific heat at constant pressure and volume (cp⁄cv)

Speed of Sound

Sound is a signal or small disturbance of fluid properties propagating in a fluid
  • The speed of sound is the speed of these small disturbances (m⁄s)
    • Since disturbances are small there is negligible heat transfer and can be assumed as isentropic
    • c = √(dp⁄dρ) = √(kRT)
    • cair = 330 m⁄s
    • cwater = 1560 m⁄s
    • kair = 1.4
  • Mach Number (M)
    • M = v⁄c
    • M < 1, subsonic
    • M > 1, supersonic
